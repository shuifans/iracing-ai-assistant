# Understanding Brake Traces To Be Faster with Delta

In the world of sim racing, data acquisition is crucial for extracting maximum potential. Braking is arguably one of the most difficult driving skills for finding lap time. In Coach Dave Delta, viewing your brake traces is easy via live data, offering valuable insights into performance. Analysing these traces can help sim racers improve braking technique and gain lap time.

## What Is The Brake Trace?

The Brake Trace represents how much brake pressure a driver applies over the position of a lap. Coach Dave Delta seamlessly imports live telemetry data into easy-to-read channel reports, showing timing and pressure while making it easy to compare reference laps from professional Coach Dave sim drivers.

The X-axis usually indicates time or distance on the track, while the Y-axis indicates brake pressure (expressed as a percentage). The shape of the brake trace can reveal a lot about the driver's braking style, including aggressiveness, smoothness, and consistency. The timing can easily show if a driver is overbraking, underbraking, or braking too early or too late.

A well-formed brake trace shows controlled, deliberate pressure, with appropriate modulation and release at the correct points. The shape can highlight erratic or inconsistent traces that may indicate errors such as locking up, over-braking, or under-braking.

Coach Dave Delta also specifically provides data on the percentages of brake pressure along the braking phase, which can help determine lock-ups and other potential braking issues.

## How To Analyse Initial Brake Application

Initial brake application is one of the most crucial elements in the braking zone. Top-level drivers apply the brakes firmly and quickly to their peak level where maximum deceleration is needed. This can be seen easily in the brake trace as a sharp rise in the initial slope.

Coach Dave Delta allows you to easily compare the rate at which you reach maximum braking pressure against a reference lap. If the application is too gradual, it can indicate a lack of confidence in the braking zone or a lack of confidence in braking reference markers. The aim should be a smooth but rapid initial application, reaching peak brake pressure quickly while keeping the car stable.

At the peak of the braking curve, look for juddering to identify where the car’s ABS may be engaging too aggressively, which can extend braking zones and reduce the car's ability to turn. The duration of the peak where a driver is on maximum braking can also identify where a driver has been on the brakes for too long compared to a proper reference lap.

> *Note: The driver’s initial brake application is too hard and too long compared to the reference.*

The initial braking phase is also where it can be seen if the driver is properly threshold braking, which is a sign that either the driver needs to work on their technique or confirm that their pedals are configured properly to ensure they are getting maximum braking pressure easily.

## Braking Through The Middle Of The Corner

After the initial pedal application, there should be a gradual reduction of brake pressure as the car approaches the corner. This allows the driver to manage the car's weight as it turns, aiming for a smooth pedal release.

This can be seen in Coach Dave Delta based on the slope of the deceleration curve at the end of the brake trace. If the trace suddenly drops off near the end of the braking compared to the reference lap, the driver may be releasing the brakes too quickly. This can destabilise the car as it turns into the corner (especially as downforce loads decrease in cars with significant aerodynamics).

> *Note: The driver got on the brakes too early, was inconsistent on max brake pressure, and then released the brakes too soon, compared to the reference lap.*

The driver may also lack confidence in trusting the car's speed as it enters the corner, slightly panicking as they come off the brakes. The objective should be a controlled and consistent reduction of braking pressure that matches the car's deceleration and weight transfer loads.

## How To Identify Trail Braking

Trail braking is the ability to gradually reduce brake pressure as the car turns into a corner, allowing the driver to carry more speed while keeping the front tyres loaded for better turn-in. In Coach Dave Delta, this can be seen as the brake trace tapers off near the end of the braking event, where pressure is reduced rather than a complete lift of the brakes.

> *Note: The driver stayed on 100% braking too long compared to the reference lap, and then released the brakes too quickly at the end of the braking phase.*

Professional reference laps show excellent trail braking references, though this is also dependent on the type of car and its setup. However, Coach Dave Academy setups provide the driver with the confidence to brake late and trail into corners.

Examine your trail braking segments by comparing your brake trace with your steering input trace. The brake pressure should gradually taper off as steering input increases.
